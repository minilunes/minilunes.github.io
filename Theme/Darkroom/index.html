Apperanace Change

The darkroom is where all the magic happens; it's a room dedicated to the development of your image and roll of film. You have to use amber or red light or else your supplies will be exposed to photo-burning light. After developing a roll of film in complete darkness, you are able to take a strip of film (usually in 4-5 frame snippets. From there, you take the snippet onto an easel, a mechanism that affixes itself into the enlarger; a light is shone through from the top-down via a lightbulb and creates the imagery onto the platform as you see in the image above.

Once you have your image established through the light and focused, you are able to place a piece of photosensitive paper (looks like regular printer paper but once exposed to any sort of light, will immediately begin to turn black/brown), onto the platform and expose your image onto it. You have to establish the percect amount of light and the perfect exposure time to create your image with enough constrast and brightness. It will first appear white until you dunk it in developer.

Developer is a mostly ammonia based chemical that takes up to 90 seconds to reveal a perfect image.

After the develper process, you dunk it in rinse. It is again, mostly water that halts the development process.

After the rinse, you dunk the image in fixer. It is a very stinky chemical that permeates your image, and makes it completely safe for light. At this point, you are able to take your image into a normally lit room where you can decide if you like it, or have to try again for better exposure, contrast, focus, etc.

After the fixer, you rinse your image once again in 100% water to rid of any excess checmials and the terrible smell left from the fixer and voila, you have your image.
